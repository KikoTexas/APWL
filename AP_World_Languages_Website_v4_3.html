CROSS-LANGUAGE DESIGN

One Architecture, Six Cultural Logics

Each of the six AP World Language courses is organized around six College Board unit themes over the year — Families and Communities, Science and Technology, and others. Rather than treating each theme as a sequence of vocabulary topics, every cycle identifies a culturally meaningful conceptual lens that gives coherence to instruction, language development, and assessment. The instructional architecture is consistent across all six courses and repeats across all six cycles; the cultural content is irreducibly distinct in every instance.

Every unit, in every cycle, follows the same three-stage developmental trajectory — moving from the personal and familiar, to how values or practices are transmitted, to their expression in the wider community — while expressing that trajectory through the social values, linguistic features, and cultural perspectives specific to each language. The table below shows Cycle 1 (Families and Communities) as one example of how this plays out; see each language tab for how the same pattern unfolds across the full year.

Shared Themes, Not a Shared Curriculum

Every course is organized around the same six College Board unit themes. Each cycle identifies a culturally significant concept within that theme, giving coherence to instruction, performance, and assessment — not a list of vocabulary topics.

Shared Conceptual Progression

Every unit moves through the same three stages: from the personal and familiar, to how values or practices are transmitted, to their expression in the wider community. Each language traces that arc through its own cultural lens.

Parallel Language Development

Linguistic complexity builds across all six cycles in parallel, course by course — from concrete, scaffolded language early in the year to extended, nuanced argumentation by Cycle 6. Every course climbs the same proficiency curve at the same pace, even as the cultural content differs.

Performance-Based Design

Every lesson builds toward one or more AP-aligned communicative performances. Interpretive, interpersonal, and presentational tasks are sequenced so students revisit core concepts in multiple modes before each cycle's culminating assessment.

Year-Long Essential Question
What happens when worlds collide?
When cultures meet, language changes, new ways of communicating emerge, and communities reshape how they see themselves, each other, and the world.
